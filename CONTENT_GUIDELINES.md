# Content Guidelines

These guidelines help authors and maintainers understand what can be published on dout.dev.

## Editorial identity

dout.dev is a community/open blog focused on web culture, frontend, design engineering, open source, accessibility, performance, UI, UX, software development, and modern digital product practices.

Tone can be personal, technical, critical, or experimental. Strong opinions are welcome; personal attacks are not.

## Relevant topics

Generally relevant:

- HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and the Web Platform
- frontend engineering
- UI engineering and design engineering
- accessibility
- performance
- UX, interaction design, and design systems
- open source
- developer experience
- AI applied to development, design, or documentation
- technical workflows and ways of working
- tutorials, case studies, retrospectives, and reasoned opinions
- tools and workflows for developers and designers
- technical culture and community

## Not relevant topics

Generally not relevant:

- purely commercial content
- posts with no connection to tech, design, or digital culture
- unedited press releases
- SEO spam
- undisclosed affiliate marketing
- political or religious propaganda unrelated to technical/cultural blog themes
- generic mass-produced content
- copied tutorials with no added value

## What makes a good article

A good article should have:

- a clear thesis
- an implied audience
- concrete examples
- sources or references when needed
- tested code, if present
- clear distinction between opinion and facts
- useful conclusion
- non-misleading title
- social-sharing-ready description

## Suggested format

Base structure:

```md
# Title

Short introduction.

## Context

Why this topic matters.

## Main point

Idea, tutorial, case study, or opinion.

## Examples

Code, images, diagrams, or practical steps.

## Limits

What this article does not cover.

## Conclusion

Clear takeaway.

## References

Links and credits.
```

## Code in articles

Code must be:

- readable
- essential
- coherent with topic
- explained
- tested or explicitly marked as pseudocode
- free of secrets, tokens, API keys, or credentials

When possible, prefer small progressive examples.

## Images, screenshots, and assets

Use only images you are allowed to publish.

For non-original images, verify:

- license
- attribution requirements
- permissions
- trademarks or people represented
- alt text accessibility

Every meaningful image must include alt text.

## Sources and attribution

Always cite:

- reference articles
- documentation
- repositories
- demos
- images
- non-original snippets
- recognizable ideas or quotes

Do not present others' work as your own.

## AI disclosure

AI tools are allowed as support, but author remains responsible.

Disclose relevant AI usage when:

- text was significantly generated
- examples or code were significantly generated
- article is a substantial rework of AI output

Example:

```md
Editorial note: this article was written with AI support and reviewed by the author.
```

## Opinions, critiques, and comparisons

Critiques are welcome when useful.

Avoid:

- personal attacks
- ridicule
- flame bait against communities or frameworks
- offensive generalizations
- empty rage bait

Prefer:

- technical reasoning
- context
- trade-offs
- examples
- alternatives
- respect for different choices

## Rejection or requested changes

An article may receive requested changes when:

- it is not clear enough
- it is not relevant
- key sources are missing
- obvious technical errors are present
- tone is aggressive or discriminatory
- there are copyright issues
- self-promotion is excessive
- moderation risk is unsustainable

A rejection must be reasoned.

## Updates to published articles

Published articles can be updated to:

- fix errors
- update links
- improve examples
- clarify passages
- add editorial notes
- correct outdated information

Major updates should be tracked in PR.

## Removal of published articles

An article can be removed or hidden if it:

- violates the Code of Conduct
- violates copyright
- exposes personal data
- contains harmful or dangerous information
- is requested for removal by author and applicable license allows it
- is considered harmful to the community by maintainers

Removal should be documented when possible.
